\section{Measurement: Born's Rule from Structural Extremum}

\textbf{v2.0:} Born's rule is \textit{derived from different axioms},
not derived from nothing. This is an axiom trade, not axiom elimination.

\textbf{v2.0 critical correction:} The v1.0 claim that Born's rule arises
from ``maximizing $\sum_k p_k |\alpha_k|^2$'' is \textbf{mathematically
incorrect}. That functional is linear in $p_k$; its maximum on the
probability simplex is a vertex (all weight on the largest $|\alpha_k|^2$),
not Born's rule. The extremization claim has been removed.

Born's rule follows from Gleason's theorem given structural constraints,
not from an optimization.

Standard QM postulates Born's rule directly: $p_k = |\langle \psi_k | \psi_S \rangle|^2$.

The UFCP replaces this with four structural constraints:

\begin{axiom}[Measurement Constraints]
\label{ax:measurement}
Let $\{\psi_k\}$ be orthonormal detector modes in a Hilbert space of
dimension $d \geq 3$, and $\alpha_k = \langle \psi_k | \psi_S \rangle$.
The detection probabilities $\{p_k\}$ satisfy:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(M\arabic*)]
  \item \textbf{Norm conservation:} $\sum_k p_k = 1$
  \item \textbf{Unitary invariance:} $p_k$ depends only on $|\alpha_k|^2$,
        not on global phases
  \item \textbf{Orthogonal additivity:} For any decomposition of the
        Hilbert space into orthogonal subspaces, probabilities assigned
        to the subspace equal the sum of probabilities of the modes
        within it
  \item \textbf{Non-contextuality:} $p_k$ for mode $\psi_k$ does not
        depend on which other modes are included in the detector basis
\end{enumerate}
\end{axiom}

\begin{theorem}[Born's Rule from Gleason]
\label{thm:born}
In a Hilbert space of dimension $d \geq 3$, the unique probability measure
satisfying (M1)--(M4) is:
\begin{equation}
  p_k = \frac{|\alpha_k|^2}{\sum_m |\alpha_m|^2}
\end{equation}
For a complete detector ($\sum_m |\alpha_m|^2 = 1$), this is the standard
Born rule $p_k = |\alpha_k|^2$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof]
By Gleason's theorem (1957): in a Hilbert space of dimension $\geq 3$,
any probability measure $\mu$ on the lattice of closed subspaces that is
(i)~countably additive on orthogonal projections and (ii)~normalized
($\mu(\mathcal{H}) = 1$) has the form $\mu(P) = \mathrm{tr}(\rho\, P)$
for a unique density operator $\rho$.

Constraints (M1) and (M3) provide the additivity and normalization
required by Gleason. Constraint (M2) restricts to pure-state measures
$\rho = |\psi_S\rangle\langle\psi_S|$. Constraint (M4) (non-contextuality)
ensures the measure depends only on the projection, not the basis.

Applying Gleason's result:
$p_k = \mathrm{tr}(|\psi_S\rangle\langle\psi_S|\cdot|\psi_k\rangle\langle\psi_k|)
= |\langle\psi_k|\psi_S\rangle|^2 = |\alpha_k|^2$.

This is exact and unique. No extremization is involved.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Axiom Trade Assessment]
Standard QM postulates Born's rule as: ``the probability of outcome $k$
is $|\alpha_k|^2$.'' The UFCP replaces this with four structural
constraints and invokes Gleason's theorem. The constraints are geometric
(additivity, invariance, non-contextuality) rather than probabilistic
(``the probability is...'').

Whether this constitutes explanatory progress: the UFCP does not explain
\textit{why} probabilities arise in a deterministic field theory. It
explains \textit{which} probability rule must hold given structural
assumptions about measurement. This is a narrower but mathematically
precise claim.

The dimension restriction ($d \geq 3$) is inherited from Gleason's
theorem. In $d = 2$ (single qubit), the constraints do not uniquely
determine Born's rule --- additional structure is needed. This is a
known limitation, not a flaw: all physical Hilbert spaces have $d \geq 3$.
\end{remark}
